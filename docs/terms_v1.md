## What egosyntonic is, and what it is not

egosyntonic is a **reflective journaling app**. It uses long-term memory of what you've written to help you notice patterns in your own thoughts and experiences. It is designed with awareness of eating-disorder and broader mental-health-adjacent content.

**egosyntonic is not therapy.** It is not a substitute for a therapist, counselor, psychiatrist, dietitian, or any other clinician. Nothing in the app is medical advice. The app cannot and does not diagnose any condition. The app cannot and does not treat any condition.

**egosyntonic is not a crisis service.** If you are in crisis, thinking about hurting yourself, or in a medical emergency, the app is not the right tool. Please reach:

- **988** (call or text) — Suicide and Crisis Lifeline.
- Text **HOME** to **741741** — Crisis Text Line.
- **911** — for medical emergencies.

**egosyntonic is not a medical device.** It is not approved or cleared by the FDA or any other regulator as a medical device. It is a consumer wellness tool.

If you are receiving care from a treatment team, please continue. Show this app to your treatment team if it helps. Don't use this app instead of your treatment team.

## Important notes about how the app responds

The app uses a large language model (currently Google's Gemini) to read what you write and produce responses. A few things to know about that:

- **The responses are generated.** They are not the personal opinions of any specific person, and they are not clinical advice. Treat them as prompts for your own reflection, not as instructions.
- **Sometimes the app gets things wrong.** The app can misread what you wrote, miss something important, or surface a pattern that doesn't actually apply to you. The "What I've learned about you" view in the app lets you correct anything that isn't right.
- **Sometimes the app declines to engage.** If you ask the app to do something it shouldn't — like give you a calorie target, talk about methods of self-harm, or diagnose a condition — it will refuse and, where appropriate, suggest reaching out to a clinician or crisis service. These refusals are part of the design.
