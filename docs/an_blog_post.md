### Egosyntonia and the Disordered Self in Anorexia Nervosa

One of the most confusing things about anorexia nervosa (AN) is that the people who have it often don't experience it as a problem. Unlike most illnesses, which produce unwanted suffering, AN occupies a paradoxical space where symptoms are simultaneously destructive and deeply valued by the person living with them. This quality in psychology is termed egosyntonia. A symptom is egosyntonic when it feels consistent with a person's sense of self, their goals, and their values, rather than feeling like an intrusion or something foreign. In AN, the restrictive eating, the rigid routines, the weight loss: these don't feel like symptoms to the person experiencing them. They can feel like accomplishments.

Understanding egosyntonia is not just an academic exercise. It may be central to understanding why AN is so resistant to treatment, and why the people who struggle with it deserve more compassion than they typically receive.

What Anorexia Actually Does for People

When researchers asked patients with AN to write letters to their disorder, once addressing it as a friend and once as an enemy, the results were telling (Serpell et al., 1999). The most common reason patients valued their AN was not about appearance or thinness, as popular accounts might suggest. Instead, patients consistently described the disorder as a “guardian”: a source of safety, protection, and dependability. Other valued functions included a sense of control and structure, the ability to avoid painful emotions, and a feeling of being special or different.

The enemy letters told a different story. Patients described being consumed by constant thoughts about food, losing friendships and relationships, and feeling taken over by the disorder. Some patients endorsed both themes, feeling in control and feeling controlled, in the same set of letters. This illustrates the deep ambivalence that characterizes the experience of AN.
Later research expanded on these findings, revealing additional functions that the disorder serves (Gregertsen et al., 2017). For some, AN was a way to prompt care from others. For others, it offered a new identity or even a slow means of dying that felt less violent than suicide. Adolescents, notably, placed greater value on the guardian and attention functions compared to adults, suggesting that what value the disorder provides shifts depending on developmental stage. 

Perhaps the most important finding from this body of research came from a scale designed to measure the perceived pros and cons of AN. Stronger endorsement of the disorder's benefits was associated with more severe illness, but endorsement of its costs was not (Serpell et al., 2004). In other words, what separates the most severely ill patients from others is not that they fail to see the harm. It is that the disorder's perceived benefits feel indispensable.

Why the Disorder Feels So Necessary

To understand why AN’s benefits feel so essential, it helps to look at the deeper psychology involved. One influential model proposes that AN is fundamentally a disorder of the self (Amianto et al., 2016). Drawing on decades of research in attachment theory and developmental psychology, this model argues that people who develop AN often have underlying difficulties with core psychological functions: regulating emotions, maintaining a stable sense of identity, and experiencing their own body as something that belongs to them.

When early relationships with caregivers don't provide enough emotional security, these capacities can fail to develop fully. The result is a person who lacks an internal foundation for managing distress, understanding their own feelings, or constructing a coherent story of who they are across time. When you consider what it would be like to move through the world without a reliable sense of self, without confidence that you can handle your own emotions or that your identity is stable, the appeal of AN becomes more understandable. The disorder provides what the self cannot: predictable rules, a concrete measure of success, a clear identity as someone with discipline, and a way to numb emotions that feel unmanageable. The guardian function identified in patients' letters reflects the disorder stepping in where the self falls short, offering a substitute for the psychological security that was never fully internalized.

Why This Matters for Treatment

This understanding has direct implications for how we think about treatment and treatment resistance. Researchers have applied the transtheoretical model of change, a framework that describes how people move through stages of readiness: from not considering change, to thinking about it, to actively pursuing it. Studies using this model found that patients who began seriously considering recovery were those who increasingly recognized the costs of AN and its role in helping them avoid difficult aspects of life. But their endorsement of the disorder's benefits did not decrease, even as they moved closer to wanting change (Cockell et al., 2003).
This creates a difficult gap. Patients can intellectually know that AN is damaging their health, their relationships, and their future, while still feeling that it is the only thing holding them together. Researchers describe this as the difference between "cold" cognition, knowing something is harmful, and "hot" cognition, actually feeling its consequences in a way that motivates action (Gregertsen et al., 2017). Many patients are stuck in cold cognition, able to list the harms but emotionally unable to let go of what the disorder provides.

This is why approaches that simply educate patients about the dangers of AN or prescribe meal plans without addressing what the disorder means to them tend to fall short. If a person derives their sense of safety and identity from their eating disorder, asking them to eat more without offering something to replace those functions is unlikely to succeed.

The most promising treatment approaches recognize this. They explore the valued functions of the disorder early and explicitly, use motivational strategies calibrated to where patients actually are in their readiness for change, and help patients identify how AN conflicts with their broader personal values, not just their health, but their relationships, their goals, and the kind of life they want to live.

This understanding also reframes treatment resistance. When a patient with AN resists recovery, it is not stubbornness or noncompliance. It is the disorder functioning as the patient believes they need it to. The appropriate clinical response then is not frustration, but curiosity about what the disorder is protecting, and patience in helping the patient build a self strong enough that the disorder is no longer needed.
